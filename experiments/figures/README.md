# Figures for the final report

All figures are generated by `experiments/analysis/make_figures.py` from the
committed run artefacts under `experiments/runs/`. Re-run from repo root:

```
backend/.venv/bin/python experiments/analysis/make_figures.py
```

Both PNG (200 dpi, for slides/Word) and SVG (vector, for Overleaf/LaTeX)
variants are written next to each other. All four figures share a single
palette so a non-expert reader can follow a consistent visual grammar.

| Colour | Hex | Meaning across all figures |
|---|---|---|
| Navy       | `#3B5C7E` | the LLM classifier condition (the winning design) |
| Sage green | `#6B8E7F` | the LLM + ACFE-Enron taxonomy condition |
| Terracotta | `#B8694A` | the heuristic baseline, and "difference is real" (p<0.05) |
| Ochre      | `#C9A876` | CoT variant, and statistical reference annotations |
| Cream      | `#F5EFE6` | figure background |
| Ink        | `#2E2E2E` | axes and body text |

Palette was adapted from the reference figure the team was working from,
chosen for print-friendliness and colour-blind legibility.

## Figure 1 — `fig1_f1_with_ci.png`
**"Which design choices actually moved the F1 score?"**

Horizontal bar chart of all ten runs with 95% bootstrap confidence intervals
(1,000 resamples on the 1,887 paired emails). Bars are coloured by condition
family. The 2.65% midterm-presentation baseline is a vertical dashed line.

*How to read it:* the two terracotta bars at the bottom of the F1 axis are
the "privacy destroyed capability" story from the midterm. The navy and
sage bars show the modern-classifier reality — 15× the baseline, with the
privacy variants (full-scrub, raw, pseudonym) indistinguishable from each
other.

## Figure 2 — `fig2_mcnemar_heatmap.png`
**"Which pairs of runs are statistically different?"**

Pairwise McNemar exact-test p-values across all ten runs. Green cells mean
the two runs disagree on enough emails that the difference is unlikely to
be chance (p<0.05). Ochre/brown cells mean the two runs are statistically
indistinguishable at this sample size.

*How to read it:* the two green columns on the left show every modern run
is significantly better than the heuristic. The absence of green anywhere
else shows that none of the privacy or prompt variants, on their own, moves
the needle enough to clear significance at n=1,887. This is the visual
version of the "H2 null / H3 under-powered" story.

## Figure 3 — `fig3_calibration_and_pr.png`
**"Is the LLM's confidence trustworthy?" (left) + "Operating-point choice" (right)**

Two-panel figure.

- LEFT: reliability diagram for E1-LLMcls. x-axis is the LLM's self-reported
  P(threat); y-axis is the empirical fraction of true positives in that
  bin. Dot size is proportional to bin count. Diagonal is perfect
  calibration; ECE (Expected Calibration Error) is annotated in-panel.
- RIGHT: precision-recall curve over the same probability scores, with the
  default threshold (0.7) and the ensemble triage threshold (0.2) marked.

*How to read it:* the left panel tells governance readers "the probability
numbers the LLM prints are meaningful — when it says 70%, about 70% of
those emails really are threats". The right panel shows the *operating*
decision: do you want few false alarms (threshold 0.7) or high recall
(threshold 0.2 for human-in-the-loop triage)? This is a governance choice,
not an accuracy choice.

## Figure 4 — `fig4_test_retest.png`
**"How much does the same LLM disagree with itself between runs?"**

Dot plot of F1 across two E1-LLMcls replicates (navy) and three E2-taxon
replicates (sage). Horizontal lines mark each group's mean; annotations
call out the SD.

*How to read it:* the two navy dots overlap exactly — the generic prompt
is perfectly reproducible at this sample size. The three sage dots scatter
over ~4 points of F1 — the taxonomy prompt has real run-to-run sampling
noise. The "+4.1 pp taxonomy effect" is therefore about 2× the size of the
taxonomy prompt's own noise, which is why H3 is reported as "consistent
but under-powered" rather than "confirmed".
